Port Owen

Port Owen is a small suburb nestled between Velddrif and Laaiplek, along a man made marina development offering 3.5 km of private waterways in a horseshoe shape with two exits to the Berg River, just a short sail to the rivermouth into St Helena Bay. It consists of private homes, sectional title flats and popular timeshare blocks. Over the past years the suburb has shown excellent growth in property values and is popular with yachtsmen, powerboaters, fishermen, birdlovers and those simple seeking a tranquil lifestyle far from the bustle of Cape Town yet serviced by a selection of shops, doctors, shools, restaurants and a short trip to the sandy beaches of Laaiplek. Port Owen includes the upmarket Admiral Island, surrounded by the marina on three sides and the Berg River to the south with CCTV cameras along the river bank, and access via controlled manned security booth with the road leading over the marina to the housing development, secure, peaceful, the perfect place to call home.


Velddrif

The village of Velddrif is situated about two kilometers inland from the Berg Rivermouth into St Helena Bay, first discovered by Vasco da Gama in 1497 in a search for fresh water. In 1693 a Dutch ship, Die Gouden Buys, ran aground south of the river mouth with two survivors being found by a Hottentot clan. The Dutch authorities were notified and ships were sent to rescue the two seamen and salvage the cargo. These travelers explored the plentiful fishing in the Bay, and over the next few years people from the Cape started visiting to explore this fishing haven. A farmer named Theunis Smit then settled here and started using the drift to access grazing fields (veld) with the town being established in 1946 and becoming a municipality in 1960. The Carinus Bridge was completed in 1959 to replace the pont that had been put in place, and is still the gateway to Velddrif, and the finishing line for the now famous annual Berg River Canoe marathon.

Laaiplek

The town of Laaiplek was founded by a European named Carl Stephan who heard about the good fishing in the Bay, and he and his brothers settled at the Berg River mouth and soon had a shipping, fishing and farming business going. Laaiplek (loading place) became the harbour and ships navigated their way around treacherous sandbanks into the Berg River. In 1968 the Cape authorities blew a channel through the sandbank at the rivermouth and the formal fishing harbour of Laaiplek was completed. The famous “Daar kom die Alibama” folksong originated from the ship coming into Laaiplek to load “riet” (reed) to take to Knysna and surrounds to thatch early settlers’ homes, with remnants of the old jetty off the beach. The fishing industry here flourished with the main industry being canning factories and making of fish meal. The main centre of the town with supermarkets, shops and doctors.
